The Problem

Today's RF Systems Are a Critical Bottleneck

Our autonomous future is constrained by RF systems architected for the last century. Legacy radar and communications operate as isolated architectures competing for scarce spectrum rather than cooperating within it. Data is siloed, latency is high, and the spectrum is saturated. This systemic inefficiency has become a critical barrier to autonomous capability — from vehicles and smart cities to defence and security — increasing vulnerability and delaying the introduction of new capabilities.

Core Challenges

The SWaP Barrier

Legacy radar, comms, and electronic warfare (EW) systems rely on separate hardware stacks, driving excessive bulk, weight, and power consumption (SWaP). This architectural inefficiency limits the scalability of autonomous and spaceborne platforms.

Spectral Inefficiency

Independent frequency allocations for sensing and communications cause spectral congestion, restrict throughput, and increase mutual interference — reducing resilience and slowing the deployment of new capabilities.

Energy-Unaware AI

Today's AI models prioritize raw performance, "overlooking... electricity power consumption". This makes them unusable on defence frontline platforms constrained by energy and size and limits applicability on autonomous systems.

Why It Matters Now

The world is moving toward autonomy, but RF systems haven’t kept up. Spectrum is crowded, data is fragmented, and sensing and communications still operate in isolation. The next decade belongs to those who can fuse them — seamlessly, intelligently, and efficiently. Dense electromagnetic environments and the rise of autonomy demand new thinking: single-function nodes can no longer perform at the competitive edge. Autonomy and defence now depend on systems that are intelligent, multi-function, cooperative, and connected.
